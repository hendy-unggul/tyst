THREAT MODEL

what tyst protects against · and what it does not
// honest disclaimer
No system is perfect. This document exists because we believe you deserve to know exactly what TYST can and cannot protect you from before you use it. If our limitations don't match your threat model, don't use TYST. Use something better suited to your needs.

We will update this document when our threat model changes. Last updated: 2026-05-08
// what tyst protects against
✓
server compromise
An attacker who gains full access to our server gets only ciphertext. Messages are encrypted on your device before leaving. Our server never holds plaintext, ever.
✓
sender identity linkage
The server does not log your IP. Messages are forwarded via relay. A random 2–47 second delay breaks timing correlation. We cannot tell you who sent a message — and we mean that technically, not just as policy.
✓
message size analysis
All messages are padded to a multiple of 256 bytes before encryption. An observer watching traffic cannot determine message length from ciphertext size.
✓
message retention
Messages are destroyed from the server 5 seconds after the recipient opens them. There is no backup. No log. Once destroyed, not even we can retrieve them.
✓
identity collection
We collect no phone number, email address, or government ID. A username is the only identifier. It is not linked to any real-world identity by us.
✓
legal compulsion — content
If legally compelled to hand over message content, we cannot. We hold only ciphertext. The recipient's private key — which we never have — is required for decryption. Finnish law governs our jurisdiction.
// what tyst does NOT protect against
✗
device compromise
Your private key is stored in localStorage. Anyone with physical or remote access to your device can extract it. TYST protects against server-side attacks, not device-side attacks. If your device is compromised, assume all messages are compromised.
✗
recipient betrayal
The recipient can screenshot, photograph, or copy any message before it self-destructs. We cannot prevent this. Trust your recipient, not just the technology.
✗
network-level surveillance
TYST does not use onion routing. A network observer can see that you are connecting to tyst.site, even if they cannot read message content. For network-level anonymity, use Tor or a trusted VPN before connecting to TYST.
✗
quantum attacks
ECDH P-256 is not post-quantum secure. A sufficiently advanced quantum computer could break the key exchange. This is a theoretical risk today, not a practical one. We will implement post-quantum hybrid cryptography in a future version.
✗
relay trust
TYST's relay is centralized and operated by us. Unlike Session (decentralized nodes) or SimpleX (self-hostable), you must trust that our relay is not logging your IP. Our canary page and Finnish jurisdiction partially mitigate this — but it is not the same as a decentralized relay. We plan to open-source the relay configuration.
✗
Third-party infrastructure dependency
During our current phase, we use Hetzner Helsinki (Finland) with Pocketbase self-hosted. Finnish jurisdiction. We depend on Hetzner as infrastructure provider — they could theoretically receive legal requests through Finnish courts. Ciphertext only is stored.
✗
formal security audit
TYST has not yet undergone a formal third-party security audit. Our crypto layer is open source and we invite community review. A formal audit (Trail of Bits, Cure53, or similar) is planned once we have the resources. Do not use TYST for high-stakes communications until this audit is complete.

// who tyst is right for
→
People who want anonymous messaging without registering a phone number or email
→
Messages that should not exist after being read
→
Sending sensitive information to someone without leaving a server-side record
→
Users who want reasonable privacy, not nation-state level protection
// who tyst is NOT right for
✗
Journalists communicating with sources in hostile jurisdictions — use SecureDrop
✗
Whistleblowers facing nation-state adversaries — use Tor + Briar
✗
Anyone whose device may be compromised or monitored
✗
High-stakes communications requiring formally audited cryptography
